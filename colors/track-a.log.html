What this session did

The colors session started from two inputs:

  • 25 INS colour entries already in the additives taxonomy, exported to colors_ins_clean.csv as a working reference
  • 44 non-INS colour variant rows from core/all_variants_working.csv — declarations found on Indian labels that had not yet been matched to an INS number

Of the 44 variant rows: 37 were resolved and removed from the working file. 5 new INS entries were added to the taxonomy. 7 rows were deferred.

Session close: 2026-03-13.

How the 37 rows were resolved

Pass 1 — Match to existing INS (25 rows, no new entries)

25 variant rows mapped to INS entries already in the taxonomy. These included FD&C-named synthetic dyes (e.g. “FD&C Red 40” → INS 129 Allura Red AC) and botanical extract declarations where the INS already existed. No new taxonomy entries were required.

Distribution of resolved variants by INS:

INS 100 (×2), 102 (×2), 110 (×3), 129 (×6), 132 (×2), 133 (×3), 160a(i) (×1), 160b (×3), 160c (×2), 171 (×1).

Pass 2 — INS exists in Codex, absent from taxonomy (6 rows, 4 new entries)

Four entries added: INS 140 (Chlorophylls), 160d (Lycopene), 161b (Lutein), and 150 (Caramel, as a single undifferentiated entry alongside the existing sub-types 150a, 150c, 150d). All four are plant-source entries.

One taxonomy addition not counted here: INS 174 (Silver, mineral) was added in Pass 4 below.

Pass 3 — Unresolvable group declarations (5 rows dropped)

artificial color, artificial colors, synthetic food colors, color vegetable juice, vegetable juice concentrate for color — no single INS number is resolvable from any of these declarations. They were removed from the working file and not added to the taxonomy.

Pass 4 — INS absent from taxonomy, mineral source (1 row, 1 entry added)

silver leaf → INS 174 (Silver, mineral). Added to taxonomy.

Why these were deferred

Each of these appears on Indian labels as a colour declaration — the product label uses one of these names in the context of declaring colour. None has an INS number. The deferral is not a classification decision; it is an acknowledgement that the current taxonomy structure has no place for them.

These substances share a structural property: they are dried, powdered, or extracted forms of whole food ingredients used for their colouring effect. They are not additives (no INS number, not in the Codex food additives framework). They are not raw ingredients in the conventional IFID sense — they are present on the label as colour declarations, not as primary ingredients.

The raw_agricultural_material lane referenced in their f_revised field does not exist in IFID. It was identified during this session as the correct home for these entries; building that category is a separate task.

The distinction paprika/carrot illustrate

INS 160c is “Paprika extract” — a solvent-extracted oleoresin from paprika, with an INS number, classified as a synthetic colour in Codex terms. The paprika row in the deferred set is the whole dried spice ground to a powder.

INS 160a(i) is “Beta-carotene, vegetable” — an extract from carrot. The carrot powder and carrot flakes rows are dried whole carrot.

These are distinct substances with distinct regulatory status. The INS extract has a Codex classification. The whole-food form has no INS number and no Codex colour classification. Both appear on Indian labels as colour declarations. The taxonomy resolves the extract; it cannot resolve the whole-food form.

Regional naming: kesar vs saffron

kesar is the Hindi name for saffron. It appears on Indian labels where an international-market label would say saffron. The two variant rows (kesar, safron) resolve to the same source substance.

safron is a spelling variant — not the correct English spelling, not the correct Hindi spelling, but a form found on labels.

This is a labeling normalisation problem, not a taxonomy gap. The deferred entry is not “kesar” as a distinct substance from saffron; it is saffron appearing under its Hindi name. Any IFID matching pipeline processing Indian labels needs to handle regional-language ingredient names alongside English and INS declarations.

The Hindi naming issue is not specific to colours. It is visible here because the colors session processed label text variants directly. The broader implication — that Indian labels use Hindi names for ingredients with no INS number — surfaces as a design question for IFID’s ingredient text matching layer.

Design question: INS number, common name, or both?

The 30 INS colour entries each carry an official_name. For plant-source and synthetic entries, the official name is the Codex GSFA name — which may not be the name on the label.

Examples:

  • INS 129: official name is “Allura Red AC.” Indian labels may say “Allura Red,” “Red 40,” “FD&C Red 40,” or in some cases just “E129” or “INS 129.”
  • INS 100: official name is “Curcumin.” Indian labels commonly say “turmeric colour” or “हल्दी” (haldi).
  • INS 160b: official name is “Annatto extracts.” Labels may say “annatto,” “bixin,” “norbixin,” or “natural colour (annatto).”

The 25 Pass 1 resolutions were precisely this problem — variant label text mapped to canonical INS entries. The mapping is in core/all_variants_working.csv (resolved rows removed) and in the Pass 1 INS distribution above, but it is not currently captured as a structured alias list in the taxonomy.

Whether IFID displays INS number, official name, common names, or all three for a colour entry is a UI question. Whether IFID stores label text aliases per entry is a schema question. The current taxonomy has the INS number and official name; it does not have a label_aliases or common_names field.
