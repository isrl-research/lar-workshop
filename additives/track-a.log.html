The design question

IFID assigns a source_type to each additive entry: synthetic, plant, mineral, or animal. That classification drives two downstream decisions — allergen and dietary flag support (halal, vegan, kosher) and origin disclosure.

The taxonomy is built. The question this report asks is: what does the distribution reveal about the classification system itself, and where does the four-category schema produce entries that can’t be cleanly placed?

Scale and session provenance

The additives taxonomy closed on 2026-03-13 with 148 entries. A subsequent colors session on the same date identified 5 INS-numbered colour entries absent from the additives taxonomy and added them: INS 140, 150, 160d, 161b, 174. The current taxonomy holds 158 entries.

The synthetic/biological tension

What the schema does

source_type: synthetic marks entries where the substance is manufactured rather than extracted. It does not distinguish between:

  • synthesis from petrochemical precursors
  • fermentation by microbial organisms
  • enzymatic conversion of plant or animal fats
  • extraction from microbial biomass

All four fall under synthetic in the current schema. The sources field is intended to carry the origin detail. But the field is empty for all synthetic entries by design — cp/README_additives.md notes that “empty sources for synthetic entries is by design; domain expert review is required.”

12 entries where the tension is explicit

12 entries in the taxonomy have source_type: synthetic but have biological, fermentation-derived, or microbial values recorded in the sources field. These were entered during the initial curation and retained as-is; the tension is documented in cp/README_additives.md as issue O-2.

Fermentation-derived, biological precursor listed:

INS Official name Sources as recorded
270 Lactic acid, L-, D- and DL- corn
296 Malic acid, DL- apple, pear
575 Glucono-delta-lactone corn
620 L-Glutamic acid molasses, corn, wheat gluten
621 Monosodium L-glutamate molasses, corn, wheat gluten

For these entries, the production route is fermentation; the listed sources are the biological substrates. The substance classified as synthetic is produced by a microbial organism from a plant-derived feedstock. The schema cannot express that both statements are true simultaneously.

Multi-origin (production route determines source):

INS Official name Sources as recorded
422 Glycerol palm, soybean, sunflower, rapeseed, lard, tallow, propylene, sugar fermentation

Glycerol’s source depends on how it was manufactured. The same INS number covers plant-derived, animal-derived, and petrochemical routes. A single source_type assignment cannot represent a substance whose origin varies by production lot.

Extracted from microbial or biological source, no abiotic route listed:

INS Official name Sources as recorded
161g Canthaxanthin algae
1100 alpha-Amylase barley malt, animal pancreas, Bacillus subtilis
1101 Protease papaya latex, papaya
1102 Glucose oxidase Aspergillus niger, Penicillium
1104 Lipases animal pancreas, fungi
1204 Pullulan starch hydrolysate from Aureobasidium pullulans

These entries list only biological or microbial sources. Their classification as synthetic appears to follow the absence of a microbial source_type in the schema.

The schema gap

The four-category source_type schema (synthetic | plant | mineral | animal) conflates production method with biological origin. A fermentation-derived substance is both synthetic (process — it is manufactured, not extracted from food) and biological (precursor — it is derived from a plant feedstock by a microbial organism). The schema can capture one dimension; it cannot capture both.

The 12 flagged entries are the entries where the tension becomes explicit. The others may carry the same tension without it being visible — if a synthetic entry has an empty sources field, there is no record of what its precursor is.

What a production_method sub-field would need to hold, based on entries already in the taxonomy:

  • petrochemical_synthesis
  • fermentation (with feedstock in sources)
  • enzymatic_conversion
  • microbial_extraction
  • plant_extraction
  • mineral_extraction

Whether to add this field, and whether to split it from source_type or replace it, is a taxonomy design decision for domain expert review. The current entries cannot populate it without a sourcing and verification pass.

What this means for IFID

IFID’s label flag support (halal, vegan, kosher) is driven by source_type: animal entries in the taxonomy. The 13 entries above are the set that triggers those flags when an additive is declared on a label.

Three notes from the distribution:

  1. INS 322 (Lecithins) is listed as source_type: animal. Lecithin is commonly derived from soy or sunflower for vegan-certified products; animal-origin lecithin (egg yolk) also exists. The single source_type assignment cannot distinguish these. A product declaring “lecithin” without source qualification cannot be flagged definitively without additional information.

  2. The fatty acid ester cluster (INS 471, 472 variants, 470, 470a) appears on a wide range of processed food labels. These are common emulsifiers. IFID’s flag logic will activate for all of them unless source qualification is added.

  3. No current FSSAI mandatory labeling requirement forces source disclosure for these additives at the ingredient level. The taxonomy carries the source data; the label often does not. The flag can be set in IFID based on the taxonomy entry; confirming it for a specific product requires knowing the production route of the specific ingredient batch.

Known issues

Three issues are documented in cp/README_additives.md and remain open:

O-1 — INS 141: plant source_type, empty sources

INS 141 (Copper complexes of chlorophylls) has source_type: plant with an empty sources field. Every other plant-type entry carries at least one source value. Whether the empty field reflects a curation gap or a genuine absence of source specificity for this entry is unresolved.

O-2 — 12 entries: source_type=synthetic with biological sources listed

Documented in the previous section. Requires a production_method design decision before it can be resolved.

O-3 — INS 296 and INS 1204: codex_category=Unclassified

Both have established Codex GSFA entries and should map to existing categories.

  • INS 296 (Malic acid, DL-): functional class acidity regulator. The Codex category is Antioxidants and acidity regulators. The DL-form is synthetic; the sources listed (apple, pear) describe the L-form origin and may have been inherited without distinguishing the isomer.
  • INS 1204 (Pullulan): functional classes include glazing agent, thickener, stabilizer, bulking agent. The Codex category is Thickeners, stabilisers, emulsifiers and gelling agents.

Both can be corrected without a sourcing pass.

The functional class multiplicity question

73 of 158 entries carry more than one functional class. An additive like INS 470 (Fatty acids) carries five: emulsifier, stabilizer, anticaking agent, antifoaming agent, glazing agent.

When a product label declares “emulsifier (INS 470),” it names one function. The taxonomy holds five. IFID’s design needs to decide: does the entry show all functional classes from the taxonomy, the declared-on-label class only, or both with the declared class flagged?

This is a display and schema question, not a curation gap. The taxonomy contains the data needed for all three options. The choice affects how labels that declare only one function for a multi-function additive are represented in IFID — and whether that representation is informative (shows all taxonomy-recorded functions) or faithful to the declaration (shows only what the label said).
