The question

IFID is a structured form. A brand person selects an ingredient, qualifies its source, specifies the process, and answers field-by-field questions — is it fortified? what functional class does it serve on this product? The form draws from a taxonomy of canonical entries; the brand makes selections from it, not freeform text.

The question this report asks: given what Indian food labels actually declare, what must the form’s fields be capable of holding? Three sessions supply the evidence. The findings document the current state of back-of-pack label declarations. The implications state what each finding signals about field type, field options, and where the current form design cannot represent what labels show.

Three closed sessions supply the evidence: the additives taxonomy session (158 entries, closed 2026-03-13), the colours session (30 INS entries, 7 deferred, closed 2026-03-13), and the CAAS label-string analysis (1,026 product rows, 2026-03-26). This report draws findings from all three, then states what follows for IFID’s design.

Findings

How additives are declared on Indian labels

88 products in the 1,026-row CAAS dataset declare at least one additive using an EU E-number. 113 products declare at least one additive using an INS number. The two sets overlap: 35 distinct additive numbers appear in both E-number and INS-number form across the dataset — the same substance, two naming conventions, different products.

E-numbers appear primarily on products from manufacturers who sell into European markets or follow European labelling conventions (Amul chocolate lines, Cadbury, Haldiram’s, Kwality Wall’s). INS numbers appear more broadly across all product categories. The pattern is not random; it follows the market orientation of the manufacturer.

The dataset shows four distinct notation variants:

Variant 1: E{n} — EU E-number, no prefix space

E322, E476, E631, E551, E330

Variant 2: INS {n} — all-caps prefix, space before number

INS 330, INS 551, INS 471, INS 102

77 products in the dataset use this variant. The most common INS form.

Variant 3: Ins {n} — mixed-case prefix, space before number

Ins 330, Ins 296, Ins 551, Ins 300

25 products use this variant. Functionally identical to Variant 2; a case-sensitive match treats them as different strings.

Variant 4: functional class or common name followed by bare number in parentheses

Colour (162), Citric Acid (330), Acidity Regulator (330), Anticaking Agent (551)

The number appears after a functional class or common name. Examples found in Doritos, Kurkure, and Lay’s ingredient strings.

A grouping pattern also appears: Acidity Regulators (Ins 296, Ins 330, Ins 334). This is not a fifth numbering convention; the numbers inside are Variant 2 or 3. The group declaration names multiple additives within one functional class.

What colour declarations look like

The taxonomy holds 30 INS colour entries: plant 16, synthetic 11, mineral 3.

Colour declarations on Indian labels draw from two distinct sources: INS-numbered colour additives, and natural whole-food materials that carry no INS number but appear on pack as colour declarations. The taxonomy currently holds only the first type. The second has no home in the current structure.

INS-numbered colour declarations

The INS colour set includes synthetic dyes, botanical extracts, and mineral pigments. FD&C names appear in this category: “FD&C Red 40” refers to INS 129 (Allura Red AC). Botanical common names also appear: a declaration for a substance is written as its common name even when an INS number exists. The alias — FD&C name, common name, or botanical name — maps to an INS entry, but this mapping is not stored in the taxonomy.

Whole-food colour sources with no INS number

The following appear on Indian labels as colour declarations and have no INS number:

Label text Source substance Note
kesar saffron Hindi/common name
paprika paprika Whole dried spice
hibiscus hibiscus Dried flower
carrot powder carrot Dried whole carrot
carrot flakes carrot Dried whole carrot
pomegranate powder pomegranate Dried whole fruit powder

The distinction between extract and whole-food form is meaningful. INS 160c is “Paprika extract” — a solvent-extracted oleoresin with a Codex classification. The paprika declaration above is the whole dried spice. INS 160a(i) is “Beta-carotene, vegetable,” an extract from carrot; carrot powder and carrot flakes are dried whole carrot. The extract has an INS number; the whole-food form does not. Both appear on Indian labels as colour declarations.

The taxonomy must be capable of retrieving both when a brand searches for a colour source. Currently it can return the extract; it has no entry for the whole-food form.

Implications for IFID

1. Additive lookup: what the form’s search field must surface

The brand person needs to find a taxonomy entry from whatever name they know the additive by. The 4 notation variants and the 35 same-substance E/INS overlaps show that a brand using Amul’s labelling convention knows INS 322 as “E322”; a brand following FSSAI convention knows it as “INS 322” or “Ins 322”; a brand who looked up their ingredient common name knows it as “Lecithin.” The form’s additive search must return INS 322 from all four entry points. This is not a parsing requirement — the brand is selecting from the taxonomy — but the form’s search index needs to cover E-number, INS-number, common name, and label alias.

The alias problem is concrete in colour declarations: FD&C Red 40, Red 40, and Allura Red all point to INS 129. The form’s search index needs to cover these aliases; the taxonomy does not currently hold them.

2. source_type as a form field

The 4-category schema (synthetic | plant | mineral | animal) is the taxonomy’s internal classification. Whether it surfaces as a form field the brand can see or edit, or whether it is read-only and set by taxonomy curation, is a design decision the data makes visible.

The 12 O-2 entries show where a single source_type cannot represent the substance. Glycerol (INS 422) is produced via plant, animal, and petrochemical routes — the same INS number, different batches. If source_type is read-only, the form shows the taxonomy classification and the brand cannot qualify it further. Whether the form should expose a per-product source qualifier — and what values it should accept — is the design question the data raises.

Whether to add a production_method sub-field — and what values it should hold — is a design decision that requires domain expert input. The current taxonomy cannot populate it.

3. Dietary flags: not a checkbox derivable from source_type alone

INS 322 (Lecithins) is source_type: animal in the taxonomy. Plant-derived lecithin from soy or sunflower is common in vegan-certified products. The taxonomy entry cannot distinguish the two. The dietary flag (halal / vegan / kosher) cannot fire automatically from source_type alone; the form must either surface the taxonomy classification and require the brand to confirm source, or expose a per-product source qualifier field.

The fatty acid ester cluster (INS 471, 472 variants, 470, 470a) carries the same issue — common emulsifiers, animal source_type in taxonomy, but plant-derived forms exist. FSSAI mandatory labelling does not require source disclosure at the ingredient level for these additives; the back-of-pack declaration does not resolve it.

4. Functional class field: single-select or multi-select?

73 of 158 entries carry more than one functional class. A brand declaring “emulsifier (INS 470)” on their label names one function; the taxonomy holds five for INS 470.

The form needs to represent two distinct things: what the taxonomy says this additive can do (all functional classes), and what function the brand is declaring on this product’s label (what is printed on the pack). If the field is single-select, the brand can only record the declared function; the taxonomy’s other classes are not surfaced. If the field is multi-select pre-populated from taxonomy, the brand must choose which apply to their product. The form design should distinguish these two representations — they answer different questions.

5. A category the form does not yet have

The 7 deferred colour rows expose a gap in the form’s category structure. Paprika, kesar/saffron, hibiscus, carrot powder — these appear on Indian labels as colour declarations. They are not additives (no INS number, not in the Codex food additives framework). In the current form structure, there is no category for them. A brand entering this product into IFID cannot select these under “additives” and there is no alternative.

The raw_agricultural_material designation was identified during the colours session as the correct home. What that category must hold, what fields it requires, and how it differs from the additive entry structure — that is a form design decision for a separate session.

Open questions

Hindi ingredient data. OpenFoodFacts cannot supply it. Of the full 19,748-row OFF export used as the CAAS source, 11 rows have Hindi ingredient text (ingredients_text_hi), 111 have Hindi product names, 2 have Hindi generic names. The 1,026-row filtered working dataset inherits this coverage profile. Any IFID work requiring Hindi ingredient text needs a different source.

INS 322 Lecithins source qualification. Should IFID store source_type at the taxonomy level only, or allow a per-product source qualifier? The current architecture has no per-product qualifier field.

INS 141 empty sources. source_type: plant, empty sources field. Every other plant-type entry carries at least one source value. Whether this is a curation gap or a genuine absence of public source data for this substance is unresolved.

Variant 4 declaration style. The pattern Functional Class (number) — e.g. Acidity Regulator (330) — appears across at least 8 products from three snack brands. Whether this reflects FSSAI-prescribed notation for certain additive categories, or is manufacturer convention, has not been verified.

O-3 fixes. INS 296 (Malic acid, DL-) and INS 1204 (Pullulan) have codex_category: Unclassified. Both have established Codex GSFA entries. INS 296 maps to Antioxidants and acidity regulators; INS 1204 maps to Thickeners, stabilisers, emulsifiers and gelling agents. Both can be corrected without a sourcing pass.
